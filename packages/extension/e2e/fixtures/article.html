The future of cross-border reading

Reading on the open web has always required a tolerance for visual chaos: navigation rails, advertising units, and persistent calls to subscribe routinely consume more of a viewport than the prose itself. Reading mode collapses that chrome and surfaces the sentence you came for.

A good reading mode does not reformat the article. It does not re-flow figures, splice paragraphs, or strip captions. It only relaxes typography — a serif body face, a comfortable measure, a generous line-height — and quietly hides every element that is not the writing.

The atomic-swap rule still applies: the page either looks like the publisher intended, or it looks like a clean reading surface. There is no flickering middle state. Enabling and disabling are single paint operations.

Localizing line-height for the script in use matters more than design discourse usually admits. Han characters and Devanagari want taller lines than Latin script; once line-height is correct the paragraph breathes.

When reading mode is the default for a domain, the user never has to ask. The sidebar fades, the ads collapse, and the only visible thing is the text — which is the only thing that ever mattered.
